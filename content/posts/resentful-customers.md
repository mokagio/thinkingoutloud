title = 'Can We Have Tech-Trattorie?'
+++

_Trattorie_ are a staple of Italian hospitality.
These little family-owned restaurants, with the dad in the kitchen, the mom at the till, and the daughters waiting at the tables, offer down-to-earth, generous, delicious meals for a reasonable price.
When you leave a trattoria, you are full to the brim and satisfied because you got _a good deal_.

Conversely, in Italy as in other parts of the world, there are restaurants where the portions are small and the prices high.
They might satiate your hunger, but you'll leave with a bad taste in your mouth and resentment over an unequal exchange.

Lately, I've felt the same resentment towards many of the tech products and services I purchase.
The price goes up, but the quality doesn't—actually, it often decreases.

Where are the trattorie of the tech industry?
Those products that are a genuine good deal?

A trattoria can offer an outstanding quality-quantity-price ratio because it's family-owned with a small staff.
It has no investors to satisfy, board members to answer to, or managers chasing bonuses.

Tech companies deliver services of complexity and scale impossible for a mom-and-pop business, but the bureaucracy necessary to sustain those achievements comes with incentives that are not aligned with offering the best service at the lowest price.

One would expect the free market to intervene at this point.
Fierce competition should level out these issues.
Yet many of the services are such that only a few companies can offer them.
Oftentimes, the best returns for founders and investors of upstart businesses that could challenge the establishment come not from competition but from acquisition.

The venture capital system has brought forth remarkable innovation and wealth creation.
It has also fostered a set of incentives for returns maximization through mechanisms other than offering a better product.
Is there a way to have one without the other?

Is the current shattering of the barriers to entry into tech via AI going to make tech-trattorie possible, or are we going to see tech giants continue to grow and squeeze more out of consumers to feed their insatiable hunger for increasing returns?
